# üî© Metals and Non-Metals

## Physical Properties

### Metals
| Property | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Lustre** | Shiny appearance when polished |
| **Malleability** | Can be hammered into sheets |
| **Ductility** | Can be drawn into wires |
| **Conductivity** | Good conductors of heat and electricity |
| **Density** | Usually high density (heavy) |
| **Melting Point** | Usually high melting points |
| **State** | Solid at room temperature (except mercury) |
| **Sound** | Produce ringing sound when struck (sonorous) |

### Non-Metals
| Property | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Lustre** | Dull appearance (no shine) |
| **Malleability** | Brittle (break easily) |
| **Ductility** | Cannot be drawn into wires |
| **Conductivity** | Poor conductors (insulators) |
| **Density** | Usually low density (light) |
| **Melting Point** | Usually low melting points |
| **State** | Can be solid, liquid, or gas |
| **Sound** | Do not produce ringing sound |

---

## Chemical Properties

### Metals
1. **React with oxygen** to form basic oxides
   $$\text{Metal} + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{Metal oxide (basic)}$$

   **Example:**
   $$4\text{Na} + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow 2\text{Na}_2\text{O}$$

2. **React with water** to form hydroxides and hydrogen
   $$\text{Metal} + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{Metal hydroxide} + \text{H}_2$$

   **Example:**
   $$2\text{Na} + 2\text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow 2\text{NaOH} + \text{H}_2 \uparrow$$

3. **React with acids** to form salts and hydrogen
   $$\text{Metal} + \text{Acid} \rightarrow \text{Salt} + \text{H}_2$$

   **Example:**
   $$\text{Zn} + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 \rightarrow \text{ZnSO}_4 + \text{H}_2 \uparrow$$

---

### Non-Metals
1. **React with oxygen** to form acidic oxides
   $$\text{Non-metal} + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{Non-metal oxide (acidic)}$$

   **Example:**
   $$\text{C} + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{CO}_2$$

2. **Do not react with water** (most non-metals)

3. **Do not react with acids** (generally)

---

## Reactivity Series of Metals

The **Reactivity Series** shows metals arranged in order of decreasing reactivity:

$$\boxed{\text{K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Pb > H > Cu > Ag > Au}}$$

**Mnemonic:** "**P**lease **S**end **C**ats **M**onkeys **A**nd **Z**ebras **I**n **L**arge **H**eavy **C**ages **S**afely **G**uarded"

- **Potassium (K)** - Most reactive
- **Sodium (Na)**
- **Calcium (Ca)**
- **Magnesium (Mg)**
- **Aluminium (Al)**
- **Zinc (Zn)**
- **Iron (Fe)**
- **Lead (Pb)**
- **Hydrogen (H)** - Reference point
- **Copper (Cu)**
- **Silver (Ag)**
- **Gold (Au)** - Least reactive

---

### Importance of Reactivity Series

1. **Predicts reactions with acids:**
   - Metals **above** H react with acids to produce hydrogen
   - Metals **below** H do not react with acids

2. **Predicts displacement reactions:**
   - More reactive metal displaces less reactive metal from its salt solution

   **Example:**
   $$\text{Zn} + \text{CuSO}_4 \rightarrow \text{ZnSO}_4 + \text{Cu}$$
   (Zinc displaces copper because Zn is more reactive)

3. **Predicts extraction methods:**
   - Very reactive metals (K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al) ‚Üí **Electrolysis**
   - Less reactive metals (Zn, Fe, Pb) ‚Üí **Reduction with carbon**
   - Least reactive metals (Cu, Ag, Au) ‚Üí Found free in nature or simple heating

# ‚ö° Extraction of Metals

## Methods of Extraction

### 1. Electrolysis (for very reactive metals)

**Used for:** K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al

**Example: Extraction of Sodium**

**Electrolysis of molten NaCl (Down's Process):**

- **Cathode (-):**
  $$\text{Na}^+ + e^- \rightarrow \text{Na}$$
  (Sodium metal is produced)

- **Anode (+):**
  $$2\text{Cl}^- \rightarrow \text{Cl}_2 + 2e^-$$
  (Chlorine gas is produced)

**Overall:**
$$2\text{NaCl} \xrightarrow{\text{electrolysis}} 2\text{Na} + \text{Cl}_2$$

---

### 2. Reduction with Carbon (for moderately reactive metals)

**Used for:** Zn, Fe, Pb

**Example: Extraction of Iron**

**Blast Furnace Process:**

1. **Iron ore (Fe‚ÇÇO‚ÇÉ)** is heated with **coke (carbon)** and **limestone (CaCO‚ÇÉ)**

2. **Reactions in the furnace:**

   a) Carbon burns in hot air:
   $$\text{C} + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{CO}_2$$

   b) Carbon dioxide is reduced to carbon monoxide:
   $$\text{CO}_2 + \text{C} \rightarrow 2\text{CO}$$

   c) Iron ore is reduced by carbon monoxide:
   $$\text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3 + 3\text{CO} \rightarrow 2\text{Fe} + 3\text{CO}_2$$

   d) Limestone decomposes:
   $$\text{CaCO}_3 \rightarrow \text{CaO} + \text{CO}_2$$

   e) Calcium oxide removes impurities (forms slag):
   $$\text{CaO} + \text{SiO}_2 \rightarrow \text{CaSiO}_3 \text{ (slag)}$$

3. **Molten iron** sinks to the bottom and is tapped off

---

### 3. Direct Heating (for least reactive metals)

**Used for:** Cu, Ag, Au (found free or in compounds that decompose easily)

**Example: Mercury**
$$\text{HgS} \xrightarrow{\text{heat}} \text{Hg} + \text{S}$$

# ü•´ Aluminum (Aluminium) - THE MOST IMPORTANT for Form 3!

## Occurrence

Aluminum is the **most abundant metal** in Earth's crust (8% by mass).

**Main ore:** **Bauxite** (Al‚ÇÇO‚ÇÉ¬∑2H‚ÇÇO or Al‚ÇÇO‚ÇÉ¬∑nH‚ÇÇO)
- Impure aluminum oxide mixed with iron(III) oxide and sand

---

## Extraction of Aluminum

### Method: **Electrolysis of Molten Alumina (Al‚ÇÇO‚ÇÉ)**

**Why not electrolysis of bauxite directly?**
- Bauxite contains impurities (iron oxide, sand)
- Must purify first to get pure aluminum oxide (alumina)

---

### Step 1: Purification of Bauxite (Bayer Process)

1. **Crush bauxite** ore into powder
2. **Heat with sodium hydroxide** under pressure:
   $$\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3 + 2\text{NaOH} \rightarrow 2\text{NaAlO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$
   (Aluminum oxide dissolves, impurities don't)

3. **Filter** to remove iron oxide and sand

4. **Dilute and cool** the solution - aluminum hydroxide precipitates:
   $$\text{NaAlO}_2 + 2\text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{Al(OH)}_3 \downarrow + \text{NaOH}$$

5. **Heat aluminum hydroxide** strongly to get pure alumina:
   $$2\text{Al(OH)}_3 \xrightarrow{\text{heat}} \text{Al}_2\text{O}_3 + 3\text{H}_2\text{O}$$

---

### Step 2: Electrolysis of Molten Alumina (Hall-H√©roult Process)

**Setup:**
- **Electrolyte:** Molten aluminum oxide (Al‚ÇÇO‚ÇÉ) dissolved in molten **cryolite** (Na‚ÇÉAlF‚ÇÜ)
- **Temperature:** About 900¬∞C (cryolite lowers melting point from 2050¬∞C)
- **Cathode (-):** Carbon lining of steel cell
- **Anode (+):** Large carbon (graphite) blocks

**Why use cryolite?**
1. Lowers melting point of alumina (saves energy and cost)
2. Makes aluminum oxide conduct electricity better
3. Acts as a solvent for aluminum oxide

---

### Electrode Reactions

**At the Cathode (-):**
$$\text{Al}^{3+} + 3e^- \rightarrow \text{Al}$$
- Aluminum ions gain electrons
- Liquid aluminum metal forms and sinks to bottom

**At the Anode (+):**
$$2\text{O}^{2-} \rightarrow \text{O}_2 + 4e^-$$
- Oxide ions lose electrons
- Oxygen gas is produced

**But oxygen reacts with carbon anode:**
$$\text{C} + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{CO}_2 \uparrow$$

**This means:**
- Carbon anodes are gradually **burnt away** (consumed)
- Anodes must be **replaced regularly**
- This is called **"Moulting"** - the gradual wearing away/burning of the carbon anodes

---

### Overall Equation

$$2\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3 \xrightarrow{\text{electrolysis}} 4\text{Al} + 3\text{O}_2$$

But since oxygen reacts with carbon:
$$2\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3 + 3\text{C} \rightarrow 4\text{Al} + 3\text{CO}_2 \uparrow$$

---

### Why Electrolysis (Not Reduction with Carbon)?

**Aluminum is very reactive:**
- It's above carbon in the reactivity series
- Carbon cannot reduce aluminum oxide to aluminum
- Electrolysis is the only method that works

**Equation that DOESN'T work:**
$$\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3 + \text{C} \nrightarrow \text{Al} + \text{CO}$$
‚ùå This reaction does not occur!

---

## Properties of Aluminum

### Physical Properties

| Property | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Appearance** | Silvery-white, shiny metal |
| **Density** | Low (2.7 g/cm¬≥) - light metal |
| **Melting Point** | 660¬∞C |
| **Malleability** | Very malleable (can be rolled into thin foil) |
| **Ductility** | Very ductile (can be drawn into wires) |
| **Conductivity** | Good conductor of heat and electricity |
| **Reactivity** | Reactive, but protected by oxide layer |

---

### Chemical Properties

#### 1. Reaction with Oxygen

Aluminum reacts quickly with oxygen to form a **thin, protective oxide layer**:

$$4\text{Al} + 3\text{O}_2 \rightarrow 2\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3$$

**This oxide layer:**
- Forms immediately when aluminum is exposed to air
- Protects the metal from further corrosion
- Makes aluminum appear less reactive than it actually is
- Is why aluminum doesn't corrode like iron (rust)

---

#### 2. Reaction with Water

**Cold water:** No reaction (protected by oxide layer)

**Steam:** Reacts when oxide layer is removed:
$$2\text{Al} + 3\text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{Al}_2\text{O}_3 + 3\text{H}_2 \uparrow$$

---

#### 3. Reaction with Acids

**With dilute HCl:**
$$2\text{Al} + 6\text{HCl} \rightarrow 2\text{AlCl}_3 + 3\text{H}_2 \uparrow$$

**With dilute H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ:**
$$2\text{Al} + 3\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 \rightarrow \text{Al}_2(\text{SO}_4)_3 + 3\text{H}_2 \uparrow$$

---

#### 4. Reaction with Alkalis

Aluminum is **amphoteric** (reacts with both acids AND bases):

**With sodium hydroxide:**
$$2\text{Al} + 2\text{NaOH} + 2\text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow 2\text{NaAlO}_2 + 3\text{H}_2 \uparrow$$

---

#### 5. Thermit Reaction (Very Important!)

Aluminum is a **powerful reducing agent**. It can reduce iron oxide:

$$\text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3 + 2\text{Al} \rightarrow 2\text{Fe} + \text{Al}_2\text{O}_3 + \text{Heat}$$

**Uses:**
- Welding railway lines
- Repairing large iron/steel structures
- Emergency welding

**This reaction:**
- Is HIGHLY exothermic (produces intense heat ~2500¬∞C)
- Produces molten iron
- Is used in the **thermit process**

---

## Alloys of Aluminum

### 1. Duralumin
**Composition:** Al (95%) + Cu (4%) + Mg (0.5%) + Mn (0.5%)

**Properties:**
- Stronger than pure aluminum
- Still lightweight
- Resistant to corrosion

**Uses:**
- Aircraft bodies
- Vehicle parts
- Window frames

---

### 2. Magnalium
**Composition:** Al (70-95%) + Mg (5-30%)

**Properties:**
- Even lighter than aluminum
- Strong and tough
- Good corrosion resistance

**Uses:**
- Aircraft construction
- Vehicle bodies
- Optical instruments

---

## Uses of Aluminum

| Use | Reason |
|-----|--------|
| **Cooking utensils** | Good conductor of heat, lightweight, non-toxic |
| **Aircraft bodies** | Low density (light), strong (when alloyed) |
| **Electrical cables** | Good conductor of electricity, lightweight |
| **Window frames** | Does not corrode (oxide layer), strong, light |
| **Food packaging (foil)** | Malleable, protects from light and air, non-toxic |
| **Drink cans** | Lightweight, non-toxic, recyclable, non-corrosive |
| **Car bodies** | Lightweight (fuel efficient), strong, non-corrosive |
| **Roofing sheets** | Lightweight, waterproof, doesn't corrode |

---

## Recycling of Aluminum

**Why recycle?**
1. Extraction from ore uses **HUGE amounts of electricity**
2. Recycling saves **95% of the energy** needed for extraction
3. Environmentally friendly
4. Cost-effective
5. Aluminum can be recycled indefinitely without loss of quality

**Process:**
1. Collect aluminum waste (cans, foil, etc.)
2. Sort and clean
3. Shred into small pieces
4. Melt (requires far less energy than electrolysis)
5. Cast into blocks (ingots)
6. Roll into sheets or make new products

# üß™ Acids, Bases and Salts

## Acids

### Definition
**Acids** are substances that produce hydrogen ions (H‚Å∫) when dissolved in water.

$$\text{HCl} \xrightarrow{\text{water}} \text{H}^+ + \text{Cl}^-$$

---

### Properties of Acids

1. **Taste:** Sour taste (‚ö†Ô∏è Never taste acids in lab!)
2. **pH:** Less than 7
3. **Litmus:** Turn blue litmus paper **red**
4. **Conduct electricity** when dissolved in water (contain ions)
5. **React with metals** to produce hydrogen gas
6. **React with carbonates** to produce carbon dioxide
7. **React with bases** to form salt and water (neutralization)

---

### Common Acids

| Acid | Formula | Common Name |
|------|---------|-------------|
| **Hydrochloric acid** | HCl | Muriatic acid (stomach acid) |
| **Sulfuric acid** | H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ | Oil of vitriol (battery acid) |
| **Nitric acid** | HNO‚ÇÉ | Aqua fortis |
| **Ethanoic acid** | CH‚ÇÉCOOH | Acetic acid (vinegar) |
| **Citric acid** | C‚ÇÜH‚ÇàO‚Çá | Found in citrus fruits |
| **Carbonic acid** | H‚ÇÇCO‚ÇÉ | In fizzy drinks |

---

### Strong vs Weak Acids

**Strong Acids** (completely ionize in water):
- HCl, H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ, HNO‚ÇÉ

**Weak Acids** (partially ionize in water):
- CH‚ÇÉCOOH, H‚ÇÇCO‚ÇÉ, citric acid

---

### Reactions of Acids

#### 1. Acid + Metal ‚Üí Salt + Hydrogen

$$\text{Zn} + 2\text{HCl} \rightarrow \text{ZnCl}_2 + \text{H}_2 \uparrow$$

**Test for hydrogen:** Burns with a **pop** sound

---

#### 2. Acid + Carbonate ‚Üí Salt + Water + Carbon Dioxide

$$\text{CaCO}_3 + 2\text{HCl} \rightarrow \text{CaCl}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{CO}_2 \uparrow$$

**Test for CO‚ÇÇ:** Turns **limewater milky**

---

#### 3. Acid + Base ‚Üí Salt + Water (Neutralization)

$$\text{HCl} + \text{NaOH} \rightarrow \text{NaCl} + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$

$$\text{H}^+ + \text{OH}^- \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{O}$$

## Bases and Alkalis

### Definition

**Base:** Substance that reacts with an acid to form salt and water
**Alkali:** Base that dissolves in water to produce hydroxide ions (OH‚Åª)

$$\text{NaOH} \xrightarrow{\text{water}} \text{Na}^+ + \text{OH}^-$$

**All alkalis are bases, but not all bases are alkalis!**

---

### Properties of Alkalis

1. **Taste:** Bitter taste (‚ö†Ô∏è Never taste!)
2. **Feel:** Soapy/slippery feel (‚ö†Ô∏è Can burn skin!)
3. **pH:** Greater than 7
4. **Litmus:** Turn red litmus paper **blue**
5. **Conduct electricity** when dissolved in water
6. **React with acids** (neutralization)
7. **React with ammonium salts** to produce ammonia

---

### Common Bases and Alkalis

|-------------|---------|------------|
| **Sodium hydroxide** | NaOH | Soluble (alkali) |
| **Potassium hydroxide** | KOH | Soluble (alkali) |

| **Ammonia solution** | NH‚ÇÉ(aq)/NH‚ÇÑOH | Soluble (alkali) |
| **Copper(II) oxide** | CuO | Insoluble (base only) |
| **Zinc oxide** | ZnO | Insoluble (base only) |
| **Magnesium oxide** | MgO | Insoluble (base only) |

---

### Reactions of Alkalis

#### 1. Alkali + Acid ‚Üí Salt + Water

$$\text{NaOH} + \text{HCl} \rightarrow \text{NaCl} + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$

---

#### 2. Alkali + Ammonium Salt ‚Üí Salt + Water + Ammonia

$$\text{NH}_4\text{Cl} + \text{NaOH} \rightarrow \text{NaCl} + \text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{NH}_3 \uparrow$$

**Test for ammonia:**

## Salts

### Definition
**Salt** is a compound formed when the hydrogen ion of an acid is replaced by a metal ion or ammonium ion.

---

### Types of Salts

#### 1. Normal Salts
All hydrogen ions replaced
- **NaCl** (from HCl)
- **K‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ** (from H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ)

#### 2. Acid Salts
Not all hydrogen ions replaced
- **NaHSO‚ÇÑ** (sodium hydrogen sulfate)
- **KHCO‚ÇÉ** (potassium hydrogen carbonate)

#### 3. Basic Salts
Contain hydroxide ions
- **Zn(OH)Cl** (zinc hydroxide chloride)

---

### Preparation of Salts

#### Method 1: Acid + Metal

**For:** Soluble salts of reactive metals (Zn, Fe, Mg)

**Example:**
$$\text{Zn} + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 \rightarrow \text{ZnSO}_4 + \text{H}_2 \uparrow$$

**Steps:**
1. Add excess metal to acid
2. Filter to remove excess metal
3. Evaporate solution to crystallize salt

---

#### Method 2: Acid + Base/Alkali (Titration)

**For:** Soluble salts from alkalis

**Example:**
$$\text{HCl} + \text{NaOH} \rightarrow \text{NaCl} + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$

**Steps:**
1. Titrate acid with alkali using indicator
2. Repeat without indicator
3. Evaporate solution to crystallize salt

---

#### Method 3: Acid + Carbonate

**For:** Soluble salts

**Example:**
$$\text{CaCO}_3 + 2\text{HCl} \rightarrow \text{CaCl}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{CO}_2 \uparrow$$

---

#### Method 4: Precipitation

**For:** Insoluble salts

**Example:**
$$\text{AgNO}_3 + \text{NaCl} \rightarrow \text{AgCl} \downarrow + \text{NaNO}_3$$

**Steps:**
1. Mix two soluble salts
2. Insoluble salt precipitates
3. Filter, wash, and dry

---

### Solubility Rules

**Soluble:**
- All nitrates (NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª)
- All sodium, potassium, ammonium salts
- Most chlorides (except AgCl, PbCl‚ÇÇ)
- Most sulfates (except BaSO‚ÇÑ, PbSO‚ÇÑ, CaSO‚ÇÑ)

**Insoluble:**
- Most carbonates (except Na‚ÇÇCO‚ÇÉ, K‚ÇÇCO‚ÇÉ, (NH‚ÇÑ)‚ÇÇCO‚ÇÉ)
- Most hydroxides (except NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)‚ÇÇ slightly)
- All sulfides (except Group 1 and NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫)

# ‚öñÔ∏è Chemical Equations

## Balancing Chemical Equations

### Step-by-Step Method

**Example:** Balance the equation for burning methane:
$$\text{CH}_4 + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{CO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$

**Step 1:** Count atoms on each side
- Left: C=1, H=4, O=2
- Right: C=1, H=2, O=3

**Step 2:** Balance atoms that appear in only one compound on each side (C is balanced, H is not)

Add coefficient to balance H:
$$\text{CH}_4 + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{CO}_2 + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$$

**Step 3:** Count again
- Left: C=1, H=4, O=2
- Right: C=1, H=4, O=4

**Step 4:** Balance remaining atoms (O):
$$\text{CH}_4 + 2\text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{CO}_2 + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$$

**Step 5:** Check all atoms are balanced ‚úì

### Practice Problems

**2. Combustion of propane:**
$$\text{C}_3\text{H}_8 + 5\text{O}_2 \rightarrow 3\text{CO}_2 + 4\text{H}_2\text{O}$$

**3. Reaction of magnesium with acid:**

## Types of Chemical Reactions

### 1. Synthesis (Combination)
$$\text{A} + \text{B} \rightarrow \text{AB}$$

**Example:**
$$2\text{H}_2 + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$$

---

### 2. Decomposition
$$\text{AB} \rightarrow \text{A} + \text{B}$$

**Example:**
$$2\text{H}_2\text{O} \xrightarrow{\text{electrolysis}} 2\text{H}_2 + \text{O}_2$$

---

### 3. Displacement
$$\text{A} + \text{BC} \rightarrow \text{AC} + \text{B}$$

**Example:**
$$\text{Zn} + \text{CuSO}_4 \rightarrow \text{ZnSO}_4 + \text{Cu}$$

---

### 4. Double Displacement (Precipitation)
$$\text{AB} + \text{CD} \rightarrow \text{AD} + \text{CB}$$

**Example:**
$$\text{AgNO}_3 + \text{NaCl} \rightarrow \text{AgCl} \downarrow + \text{NaNO}_3$$

---

### 5. Neutralization
$$\text{Acid} + \text{Base} \rightarrow \text{Salt} + \text{Water}$$

**Example:**
$$\text{HCl} + \text{NaOH} \rightarrow \text{NaCl} + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$

---

### 6. Oxidation-Reduction (Redox)

**Oxidation:** Loss of electrons (or gain of oxygen, loss of hydrogen)
**Reduction:** Gain of electrons (or loss of oxygen, gain of hydrogen)

**Example:**
$$\text{CuO} + \text{H}_2 \rightarrow \text{Cu} + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$

- CuO is **reduced** (loses oxygen)
- H‚ÇÇ is **oxidized** (gains oxygen)

**OIL RIG:** **O**xidation **I**s **L**oss, **R**eduction **I**s **G**ain (of electrons)

## Hydrocarbons

### Alkanes (Saturated hydrocarbons)

### Alkenes (Unsaturated hydrocarbons)

## Common Exam Questions

### Question Type 1: Aluminum Extraction

**Q:** Explain why cryolite is added to aluminum oxide during electrolysis. (3 marks)

**A:**
1. Lowers the melting point of aluminum oxide from 2050¬∞C to about 900¬∞C
2. Saves energy and reduces costs
3. Makes the aluminum oxide conduct electricity better

---

### Question Type 2: Moulting

**Q:** Why must the carbon anodes be replaced regularly during aluminum extraction? (2 marks)

**A:**
1. Oxygen produced at the anode reacts with carbon to form carbon dioxide
2. This causes the carbon anodes to gradually burn away (moult/wear away)

---

### Question Type 3: Reactivity Series

**Q:** Explain why zinc displaces copper from copper sulfate solution but copper cannot displace zinc from zinc sulfate. (3 marks)

**A:**
1. Zinc is more reactive than copper in the reactivity series
2. A more reactive metal can displace a less reactive metal from its salt solution
3. Copper is less reactive than zinc, so cannot displace it

---

### Question Type 4: Neutralization

**Q:** Write a balanced equation for the reaction between sulfuric acid and sodium hydroxide. Name the salt formed. (3 marks)

**A:**
$$\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 + 2\text{NaOH} \rightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_4 + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$$

Salt: Sodium sulfate
