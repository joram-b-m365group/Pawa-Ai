# ‚öõÔ∏è Atomic Structure

## Atomic Components

**The atom consists of:**

**1. Protons** ($p^+$)
- Location: Nucleus
- Charge: $+1$
- Mass: $\approx 1$ amu

**2. Neutrons** ($n^0$)
- Location: Nucleus
- Charge: $0$ (neutral)
- Mass: $\approx 1$ amu

**3. Electrons** ($e^-$)
- Location: Electron shells
- Charge: $-1$
- Mass: $\approx \frac{1}{1836}$ amu (negligible)

---

## Atomic Number and Mass Number

**Atomic Number** ($Z$):
$$\boxed{Z = \text{Number of protons}}$$

**Mass Number** ($A$):
$$\boxed{A = \text{Protons} + \text{Neutrons}}$$

**Number of neutrons:**
$$\boxed{N = A - Z}$$

**Standard notation:**
$$\boxed{^{A}_{Z}X}$$

**Example:** Carbon-12
$$^{12}_{6}C$$
- Protons: $Z = 6$
- Neutrons: $N = 12 - 6 = 6$
- Electrons: $6$ (in neutral atom)

---

## Isotopes

**Definition:** Atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons

**Example:** Carbon isotopes
- $^{12}C$ (Carbon-12): 6 protons, 6 neutrons
- $^{13}C$ (Carbon-13): 6 protons, 7 neutrons
- $^{14}C$ (Carbon-14): 6 protons, 8 neutrons (radioactive)

**Relative Atomic Mass:**
$$\boxed{A_r = \frac{\sum(\text{isotope mass} \times \text{abundance})}{100}}$$

**Example:** Chlorine
- $^{35}Cl$: 75% abundance, mass = 35
- $^{37}Cl$: 25% abundance, mass = 37

$$A_r = \frac{(35 \times 75) + (37 \times 25)}{100} = \frac{2625 + 925}{100} = 35.5$$

---

## Electron Configuration

**Rules:**
1. **Aufbau Principle:** Fill lowest energy levels first
2. **Pauli Exclusion:** Max 2 electrons per orbital
3. **Hund's Rule:** Fill orbitals singly before pairing

**Electron shell capacity:**
$$\boxed{\text{Max electrons} = 2n^2}$$

Where $n$ = shell number

**Shells:**
- $n=1$: max $2(1)^2 = 2$ electrons
- $n=2$: max $2(2)^2 = 8$ electrons
- $n=3$: max $2(3)^2 = 18$ electrons

**Examples:**

**Carbon (6 electrons):**
$$1s^2 \, 2s^2 \, 2p^2$$
or: $2, 4$ (shell notation)

**Oxygen (8 electrons):**
$$1s^2 \, 2s^2 \, 2p^4$$
or: $2, 6$

**Sodium (11 electrons):**
$$1s^2 \, 2s^2 \, 2p^6 \, 3s^1$$
or: $2, 8, 1$

# üîó Chemical Bonding

## Types of Chemical Bonds

### 1. Ionic Bonding

**Definition:** Transfer of electrons from metal to non-metal

**Formation:**
$$\text{Metal} - e^- \rightarrow \text{Metal}^+ \text{ (cation)}$$
$$\text{Non-metal} + e^- \rightarrow \text{Non-metal}^- \text{ (anion)}$$

**Example:** Sodium chloride (NaCl)
$$Na \rightarrow Na^+ + e^-$$
$$Cl + e^- \rightarrow Cl^-$$
$$Na^+ + Cl^- \rightarrow NaCl$$

**Properties:**
- High melting/boiling points
- Conduct electricity when molten/dissolved
- Form crystalline structures

---

### 2. Covalent Bonding

**Definition:** Sharing of electron pairs between non-metals

**Single bond:** Share 1 pair (2 electrons)
$$H \cdot + \cdot H \rightarrow H:H \text{ or } H-H$$

**Double bond:** Share 2 pairs (4 electrons)
$$O::O \text{ or } O=O$$

**Triple bond:** Share 3 pairs (6 electrons)
$$N:::N \text{ or } N \equiv N$$

**Examples:**

**Water** ($H_2O$):
$$H-O-H$$
2 single bonds

**Carbon dioxide** ($CO_2$):
$$O=C=O$$
2 double bonds

**Nitrogen gas** ($N_2$):
$$N \equiv N$$
1 triple bond

---

### 3. Metallic Bonding

**Definition:** "Sea of electrons" shared among metal atoms

**Structure:**
$$\text{Metal}^+ \text{ ions in sea of delocalized } e^-$$

**Properties:**
- Good electrical conductivity
- Good thermal conductivity
- Malleable and ductile
- Lustrous (shiny)

---

## Electronegativity

**Definition:** Ability to attract electrons in a bond

**Pauling scale:** 0 to 4

**Most electronegative:** F (Fluorine) = 4.0
**Least electronegative:** Fr (Francium) = 0.7

**Electronegativity difference ($\Delta EN$):**

$$\Delta EN < 0.5 \rightarrow \text{Non-polar covalent}$$
$$0.5 < \Delta EN < 1.7 \rightarrow \text{Polar covalent}$$
$$\Delta EN > 1.7 \rightarrow \text{Ionic}$$

---

## Lewis Structures

**Steps:**
1. Count total valence electrons
2. Draw skeletal structure
3. Complete octets (8 electrons)
4. Form multiple bonds if needed

**Example:** $CO_2$

**Step 1:** Total electrons
- C: 4 valence
- O: 6 valence √ó 2 = 12
- Total: $4 + 12 = 16$ electrons

**Step 2-4:** Structure
$$O=C=O$$

# ‚öóÔ∏è Chemical Reactions

## Types of Chemical Reactions

### 1. Synthesis (Combination)
$$\boxed{A + B \rightarrow AB}$$

**Example:**
$$2H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O$$

---

### 2. Decomposition
$$\boxed{AB \rightarrow A + B}$$

**Example:**
$$2H_2O \xrightarrow{\text{electricity}} 2H_2 + O_2$$

---

### 3. Single Displacement
$$\boxed{A + BC \rightarrow AC + B}$$

**Example:**
$$Zn + 2HCl \rightarrow ZnCl_2 + H_2$$

---

### 4. Double Displacement
$$\boxed{AB + CD \rightarrow AD + CB}$$

**Example:**
$$AgNO_3 + NaCl \rightarrow AgCl \downarrow + NaNO_3$$

---

### 5. Combustion
$$\boxed{\text{Fuel} + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + H_2O + \text{Energy}}$$

**Example:**
$$CH_4 + 2O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + 2H_2O$$

---

## Balancing Chemical Equations

**Law of Conservation of Mass:**
$$\boxed{\text{Mass of reactants} = \text{Mass of products}}$$

**Steps:**
1. Write unbalanced equation
2. Count atoms on each side
3. Add coefficients to balance
4. Check your work

**Example:** Combustion of propane

**Unbalanced:**
$$C_3H_8 + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + H_2O$$

**Balanced:**
$$C_3H_8 + 5O_2 \rightarrow 3CO_2 + 4H_2O$$

**Check:**
- C: $3 = 3$ ‚úì
- H: $8 = 8$ ‚úì
- O: $10 = 10$ ‚úì

# üî¥üîµ Acids, Bases & pH

## Definitions

### Arrhenius Definition

**Acid:** Produces $H^+$ in water
$$HCl \rightarrow H^+ + Cl^-$$

**Base:** Produces $OH^-$ in water
$$NaOH \rightarrow Na^+ + OH^-$$

---

### Br√∏nsted-Lowry Definition

**Acid:** Proton ($H^+$) donor
$$HA \rightarrow H^+ + A^-$$

**Base:** Proton ($H^+$) acceptor
$$B + H^+ \rightarrow BH^+$$

---

## pH Scale

**Definition:**
$$\boxed{pH = -\log[H^+]}$$

**Range:** 0 to 14

$$pH < 7 \rightarrow \text{Acidic}$$
$$pH = 7 \rightarrow \text{Neutral}$$
$$pH > 7 \rightarrow \text{Basic (Alkaline)}$$

---

## pH Calculations

**Example 1:** Find pH of $[H^+] = 1 \times 10^{-3}$ M

$$pH = -\log(1 \times 10^{-3}) = -(-3) = 3$$

**Example 2:** Find $[H^+]$ if pH = 5

$$[H^+] = 10^{-pH} = 10^{-5} = 0.00001 \text{ M}$$

---

## pOH and Relationship

$$\boxed{pOH = -\log[OH^-]}$$

$$\boxed{pH + pOH = 14}$$

$$\boxed{[H^+] \times [OH^-] = 1 \times 10^{-14}}$$

**Example:** If pH = 3, find pOH

$$pOH = 14 - 3 = 11$$

---

## Strong vs Weak Acids/Bases

**Strong acids** (100% ionized):
- $HCl$ (Hydrochloric acid)
- $H_2SO_4$ (Sulfuric acid)
- $HNO_3$ (Nitric acid)

**Weak acids** (partially ionized):
- $CH_3COOH$ (Acetic acid)
- $H_2CO_3$ (Carbonic acid)

**Strong bases:**
- $NaOH$ (Sodium hydroxide)
- $KOH$ (Potassium hydroxide)

**Weak bases:**
- $NH_3$ (Ammonia)

---

## Neutralization Reactions

$$\boxed{\text{Acid} + \text{Base} \rightarrow \text{Salt} + \text{Water}}$$

**Example:**
$$HCl + NaOH \rightarrow NaCl + H_2O$$

**Net ionic equation:**
$$H^+ + OH^- \rightarrow H_2O$$

# üßÆ The Mole Concept

## Avogadro's Number

$$\boxed{N_A = 6.022 \times 10^{23} \text{ particles/mol}}$$

**1 mole contains:** $6.022 \times 10^{23}$ particles

---

## Mole Formula

$$\boxed{n = \frac{m}{M}}$$

Where:
- $n$ = moles (mol)
- $m$ = mass (g)
- $M$ = molar mass (g/mol)

**Example:** Moles in 36g of water ($H_2O$)

**Step 1:** Molar mass
$$M = 2(1) + 16 = 18 \text{ g/mol}$$

**Step 2:** Calculate moles
$$n = \frac{36}{18} = 2 \text{ mol}$$

---

## Molar Mass Calculation

$$\boxed{M = \sum (\text{atomic mass} \times \text{number of atoms})}$$

**Examples:**

**$H_2SO_4$ (Sulfuric acid):**
$$M = 2(1) + 32 + 4(16) = 2 + 32 + 64 = 98 \text{ g/mol}$$

**$Ca(NO_3)_2$ (Calcium nitrate):**
$$M = 40 + 2[14 + 3(16)] = 40 + 2(62) = 164 \text{ g/mol}$$

---

## Conversions

**Mass ‚Üî Moles:**
$$m = n \times M$$

**Moles ‚Üî Particles:**
$$N = n \times N_A$$

**Moles ‚Üî Volume (gases at STP):**
$$V = n \times 22.4 \text{ L}$$

---

# ‚öñÔ∏è Stoichiometry

## Mole Ratios

From balanced equation: $aA + bB \rightarrow cC + dD$

$$\boxed{\frac{n_A}{a} = \frac{n_B}{b} = \frac{n_C}{c} = \frac{n_D}{d}}$$

---

## Stoichiometry Calculations

**Example:** How many grams of $H_2O$ are produced from 4g of $H_2$?

**Equation:**
$$2H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O$$

**Step 1:** Moles of $H_2$
$$n_{H_2} = \frac{4}{2} = 2 \text{ mol}$$

**Step 2:** Mole ratio
$$\frac{n_{H_2}}{2} = \frac{n_{H_2O}}{2}$$
$$n_{H_2O} = 2 \text{ mol}$$

**Step 3:** Mass of $H_2O$
$$m_{H_2O} = 2 \times 18 = 36 \text{ g}$$

---

## Limiting Reactant

**Definition:** Reactant that runs out first, limiting product formation

**Steps:**
1. Calculate moles of each reactant
2. Divide by stoichiometric coefficient
3. Smallest value = limiting reactant

**Example:**
$$N_2 + 3H_2 \rightarrow 2NH_3$$

Given: 2 mol $N_2$, 4 mol $H_2$

**For $N_2$:** $\frac{2}{1} = 2$
**For $H_2$:** $\frac{4}{3} = 1.33$ ‚Üê **Limiting!**

$H_2$ is limiting reactant

---

## Percent Yield

$$\boxed{\text{Percent Yield} = \frac{\text{Actual Yield}}{\text{Theoretical Yield}} \times 100\%}$$

**Example:**
- Theoretical: 50g
- Actual: 40g

$$\text{Yield} = \frac{40}{50} \times 100\% = 80\%$$

# üí® Gas Laws

## Ideal Gas Law

$$\boxed{PV = nRT}$$

Where:
- $P$ = Pressure (atm or kPa)
- $V$ = Volume (L)
- $n$ = Moles (mol)
- $R$ = Gas constant
  - $R = 0.0821 \text{ L¬∑atm/(mol¬∑K)}$
  - $R = 8.314 \text{ J/(mol¬∑K)}$
- $T$ = Temperature (K)

**Temperature conversion:**
$$\boxed{K = ¬∞C + 273.15}$$

---

## Boyle's Law (Constant T, n)

$$\boxed{P_1V_1 = P_2V_2}$$

**Pressure ‚àù $\frac{1}{\text{Volume}}$**

**Example:**
- $P_1 = 2$ atm, $V_1 = 5$ L
- $P_2 = ?$, $V_2 = 10$ L

$$2 \times 5 = P_2 \times 10$$
$$P_2 = 1 \text{ atm}$$

---

## Charles's Law (Constant P, n)

$$\boxed{\frac{V_1}{T_1} = \frac{V_2}{T_2}}$$

**Volume ‚àù Temperature (K)**

---

## Gay-Lussac's Law (Constant V, n)

$$\boxed{\frac{P_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2}{T_2}}$$

**Pressure ‚àù Temperature (K)**

---

## Combined Gas Law

$$\boxed{\frac{P_1V_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2V_2}{T_2}}$$

---

## Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures

$$\boxed{P_{total} = P_1 + P_2 + P_3 + \ldots}$$

**Example:**
- $P_{N_2} = 0.8$ atm
- $P_{O_2} = 0.2$ atm

$$P_{total} = 0.8 + 0.2 = 1.0 \text{ atm}$$

# üî• Thermochemistry

## Enthalpy Change

$$\boxed{\Delta H = H_{products} - H_{reactants}}$$

**Exothermic:** $\Delta H < 0$ (releases heat)
**Endothermic:** $\Delta H > 0$ (absorbs heat)

**Example:**
$$CH_4 + 2O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + 2H_2O \quad \Delta H = -890 \text{ kJ}$$

---

## Hess's Law

$$\boxed{\Delta H_{total} = \sum \Delta H_{steps}}$$

**Example:**
$$\text{Target: } C + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2$$

**Given:**
$$\begin{align}
C + \frac{1}{2}O_2 &\rightarrow CO \quad \Delta H_1 = -110 \text{ kJ} \\
CO + \frac{1}{2}O_2 &\rightarrow CO_2 \quad \Delta H_2 = -283 \text{ kJ}
\end{align}$$

**Sum:**
$$\Delta H_{total} = -110 + (-283) = -393 \text{ kJ}$$

---

## Specific Heat Capacity

$$\boxed{q = mc\Delta T}$$

Where:
- $q$ = Heat energy (J)
- $m$ = Mass (g)
- $c$ = Specific heat capacity (J/g¬∞C)
- $\Delta T$ = Temperature change (¬∞C)

**Water:** $c = 4.18$ J/g¬∞C

**Example:** Heat to warm 100g water by 10¬∞C

$$q = 100 \times 4.18 \times 10 = 4180 \text{ J} = 4.18 \text{ kJ}$$

---

# ‚öñÔ∏è Chemical Equilibrium

## Equilibrium Constant

For: $aA + bB \rightleftharpoons cC + dD$

$$\boxed{K_c = \frac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b}}$$

**Interpretation:**
- $K_c \gg 1$: Products favored
- $K_c \ll 1$: Reactants favored
- $K_c \approx 1$: Neither favored

---

## Le Chatelier's Principle

**If stress applied, equilibrium shifts to relieve stress**

**Effects:**

**1. Concentration:**
- Add reactant ‚Üí shifts right
- Remove product ‚Üí shifts right

**2. Pressure (gases):**
- Increase pressure ‚Üí shifts to fewer moles side

**3. Temperature:**
- Exothermic: Heat increase ‚Üí shifts left
- Endothermic: Heat increase ‚Üí shifts right

# ‚ö° Electrochemistry

## Oxidation-Reduction (Redox)

**Oxidation:** Loss of electrons
$$\boxed{\text{OIL: Oxidation Is Loss}}$$

**Reduction:** Gain of electrons
$$\boxed{\text{RIG: Reduction Is Gain}}$$

**Example:**
$$Zn + Cu^{2+} \rightarrow Zn^{2+} + Cu$$

- $Zn$ loses 2$e^-$ ‚Üí **Oxidized**
- $Cu^{2+}$ gains 2$e^-$ ‚Üí **Reduced**

---

## Oxidation Numbers

**Rules:**
1. Free element: 0
2. Monatomic ion: Charge value
3. O in compounds: -2 (usually)
4. H in compounds: +1 (usually)
5. Sum = charge of molecule/ion

---

## Electrochemical Cells

**Galvanic Cell:** Chemical ‚Üí Electrical energy (battery)

**Electrolytic Cell:** Electrical ‚Üí Chemical energy (electrolysis)

**Cell potential:**
$$\boxed{E¬∞_{cell} = E¬∞_{cathode} - E¬∞_{anode}}$$
